Good morning everyone. Today, I would like to share with you a beautiful story from Buddhism—the story of the birth of the Buddha. Even if you are new to Buddhism, I believe this story can still touch your heart. It is not only the story of the birth of a great spiritual teacher. It is also a story about hope, compassion, wisdom, and the possibility that every human being can overcome suffering and discover inner peace. More than 2,600 years ago, in ancient India, a child was born who would later become the Buddha, the Awakened One. 
His teachings would spread across many countries and continue to inspire millions of people even today. According to Buddhist tradition, Queen Maya became pregnant when she was around forty years old. One night, she had a very special dream. She heard beautiful heavenly music filling the sky. Then she saw a magnificent white elephant with six tusks slowly descending from above. The elephant approached her peacefully and entered her womb. The next morning, Queen Maya told King Suddhodana about the dream.
The king invited astrologers, spiritual advisers, and wise men to explain its meaning. After hearing the story, they all made a similar prediction. They said that the queen would give birth to an extraordinary child, someone who would bring great benefit to both the royal family and the world. This news filled the king with joy. Like any father, he hoped that his son would one day inherit the throne and become a great ruler. At that time, having a worthy successor was very important for the future of the kingdom. During her pregnancy, Queen Maya lived a very pure and peaceful life. Traditional Buddhist accounts describe her as living almost like a spiritual practitioner. Her thoughts were calm, her actions were gentle, and her heart was filled with goodness. Whenever I reflect on this part of the story, I feel that it teaches us something important. The environment around a child begins even before birth. When parents cultivate kindness, peace, and virtue, they create a beautiful foundation for future generations.
As the day of childbirth approached, Queen Maya prepared to return to her hometown. According to the custom of ancient India, a woman would usually give birth in her parents' home rather than in her husband's home. Therefore, King Suddhodana arranged a royal procession to accompany her back to Devadaha, the kingdom where she had grown up.
Traveling in those days was very different from today. There were no smooth roads, cars, or airplanes. People traveled by horse-drawn carts or ox carts over rough and uneven roads. For a woman close to giving birth, such a journey could not have been easy. Yet perhaps this was part of a greater destiny. The future Buddha would not be born inside a magnificent palace. Instead, he would be born in the midst of nature. As the royal procession approached Lumbini Garden, something unusual happened. A wonderful fragrance suddenly filled the air.
It spread across a wide area and drifted gently with the wind. Queen Maya immediately noticed it and asked the driver to slow down. Curious about the source of such a beautiful scent, she looked outside the cart. Soon she saw a magnificent Udumbara flower blooming among the trees. It was said to be unusually large and incredibly beautiful.
Its fragrance filled the entire area. Ancient traditions believed that whenever a great holy being appeared in the world, the Udumbara flower would bloom. Whether we understand this literally or symbolically, the message is beautiful. Nature itself seemed to be welcoming the arrival of someone who would bring wisdom and compassion to the world. The flower was so beautiful that Queen Maya wished to see it more closely. She asked her attendants to help her down from the cart and slowly walked toward it. The attendants followed quietly behind her as she entered the peaceful garden. Imagine that moment for a moment.
The trees were green and full of life. Birds sang among the branches. Gentle breezes moved through the leaves. The sweet fragrance of flowers filled the air. Everything seemed calm, peaceful, and harmonious. I often feel that there is deep meaning in the Buddha being born in a garden rather than in a palace. Today many people spend their lives surrounded by noise, stress, and endless distractions. Yet some of the most meaningful moments in life happen when we reconnect with nature. The Buddha's birth reminds us that human beings are not separate from nature. We are part of it. As Queen Maya reached toward the flower, an extraordinary event took place. According to Buddhist tradition, Prince Siddhartha was born at that moment. The future Buddha entered the world not in a palace and not surrounded by luxury, but beneath the open sky, among trees and flowers. Ancient accounts tell us that wonderful signs appeared throughout the area. A radiant light filled the sky. 
Heavenly music could be heard. Flowers seemed to fall from above like gentle rain. A sweet fragrance spread in every direction. Everyone present was amazed by what they witnessed. Tradition also tells us that gentle rain descended from the heavens, as if nature itself were offering a blessing to the newborn child. The attendants, guards, and all those who accompanied Queen Maya were filled with wonder and amazement. Of course, when we hear these stories today, some people may understand them literally while others may see them as symbolic. But regardless of how we interpret them, the deeper message remains the same. 
The birth of the Buddha was not seen as an ordinary event. It marked the arrival of a person who would one day help countless people find freedom from suffering. After the birth, there was no longer any need to continue the journey to Queen Maya's hometown. 
The royal procession turned around and slowly returned to Kapilavastu. Messengers rode ahead to bring the joyful news to King Suddhodana. When the king heard the news, he was filled with happiness. He came out with his ministers and members of the royal court to welcome the queen and the newborn prince. When he held his son in his arms, his heart overflowed with joy. The child appeared unusually beautiful, and many people felt there was something special about him. However, while the kingdom celebrated, Queen Maya's health gradually became weaker. Giving birth during a difficult journey and then traveling back over rough roads placed a great burden on her body. This reminds us that even in the stories of great spiritual figures, human life still contains challenges, sacrifice, and suffering. 
Soon after the prince's birth, King Suddhodana invited a famous spiritual sage named Asita to examine the child. Asita was highly respected for his wisdom, meditation, and spiritual insight. When he arrived at the palace, he carefully observed the prince. The king watched eagerly, hoping to hear good news. But as Asita continued looking at the child, his expression became increasingly serious. The king began to worry. Then, unexpectedly, the old sage started to cry. Imagine how surprising that must have been. Here was a man who had spent his entire life practicing spirituality and developing inner peace. Yet he suddenly burst into tears. 
King Suddhodana became frightened and quickly asked, “Is there some misfortune that will happen to my son?” Asita replied, “No, Your Majesty. There is no misfortune. This child will become one of the greatest human beings the world has ever known. If he remains in worldly life, he will become a great universal king. But if he leaves the palace and follows the spiritual path, he will become a fully enlightened Buddha who will guide countless beings.
Then Asita revealed the reason for his tears. He was not crying because of sadness for the prince. He was crying because he was already very old. He knew that he would not live long enough to hear the teachings that this child would one day share with the world. Whenever I think about this moment, I find it deeply moving. Imagine recognizing that someone will bring wisdom and hope to humanity, yet knowing that you yourself will not be there to receive those teachings. Before leaving, Asita told his nephew, “When this prince grows up and begins teaching, go and learn from him.” It was as if he could already see the future. 
Seven days after the prince was born, a naming ceremony was held. Wise men and spiritual experts were invited to examine the child. After carefully observing him, they all reached a similar conclusion. They believed that the prince would either become a great ruler or a fully enlightened Buddha. Finally, the child was given the name Siddhartha, which means “the one who achieves his goal” or “the one whose wishes are fulfilled.” Looking back on this story today, perhaps the most important lesson is not the miraculous events themselves.
The most important lesson is what this child would later become. Prince Siddhartha grew up to become the Buddha, a teacher who showed humanity a path of wisdom, compassion, peace, and awakening. His birth reminds us that every human life has value. Every person begins as a child. Every person has the potential to develop kindness, wisdom, and understanding. The Buddha was born as a human being, and because of that, his life gives hope to all of us. Thank you for listening, and may the story of the Buddha's birth inspire us to cultivate more compassion, wisdom, and peace in our own lives.

Now I would like to spend a little more time reflecting on the moment of the Buddha's birth itself, because this is one of the most meaningful and inspiring parts of the story. When Queen Maya entered Lumbini Garden, she was surrounded by a peaceful natural world. There were trees, flowers, fresh air, and the gentle sounds of nature. Everything seemed calm and harmonious.In Buddhism, this setting is very meaningful. The Buddha was not born in a palace room decorated with gold and jewels.He was not born in a place of luxury or power.
Instead, he was born in the middle of nature. I think this teaches us something beautiful. Before we belong to a nation, a culture, or a social class, we belong to this earth. We all breathe the same air, live under the same sky, and depend on nature for our lives. Perhaps this is one reason why the Buddha's birth took place in a garden. His life and teachings would later belong not only to one kingdom or one people, but to all humanity. According to Buddhist tradition, as Queen Maya reached toward the branch of a flowering tree, Prince Siddhartha was born. Ancient texts describe many extraordinary signs appearing at that moment.
A brilliant light spread throughout the sky. Heavenly music filled the air. Fragrant flowers seemed to rain down from above. Gentle showers descended from the heavens. A wonderful fragrance spread in every direction. Everyone present was amazed. The attendants, guards, and members of the royal procession looked at one another in wonder. They knew they were witnessing something extraordinary. When people hear these stories today, especially in the modern world, they sometimes ask whether these events happened exactly as described.
Different people may have different views. Some Buddhists understand these accounts literally. Others see them as symbolic descriptions created to express the greatness of the Buddha. Personally, I think the deeper meaning is more important than the physical details. Whether the flowers literally fell from the sky or not, the message is that the birth of the Buddha brought beauty, hope, and blessings into the world. Whether heavenly music literally filled the air or not, the message is that something profoundly joyful had occurred. Sometimes, when a truly great person appears in history, ordinary words are not enough to describe their importance. People naturally use beautiful symbols and poetic language to express their feelings. The story of the Buddha's birth can be understood in this way. One of the most famous traditions tells us that after being born, the infant prince took several steps forward. 
As he walked, flowers appeared beneath his feet. Again, people may understand this story differently. But the symbolism is powerful. A lotus flower grows in muddy water, yet it rises above the mud and blossoms into something pure and beautiful. For this reason, the lotus became one of the most important symbols in Buddhism. It reminds us that even though we live in a world that contains difficulties, confusion, and suffering, we still have the ability to develop wisdom and compassion. Just as a lotus rises from mud, a human being can rise above greed, anger, and ignorance. When I think about this symbol, I find it very encouraging. 
Many of us sometimes feel discouraged by our mistakes or weaknesses. We may think that because our lives are imperfect, we cannot become better people. But the lotus teaches the opposite lesson. Its beauty comes precisely because it grows in difficult conditions. In the same way, many of our greatest lessons come from challenges, failures, and hardships. The Buddha's life would later demonstrate this truth again and again. Another tradition tells us that the young prince made a special declaration immediately after his birth. The exact words have been recorded differently throughout history, and scholars continue to discuss their meaning. 
What is important is that this declaration symbolized a future unlike any other. The child who had just entered the world would one day discover the path to enlightenment and share it with countless beings. At that moment, no one could fully understand what lay ahead. The attendants saw only a newborn baby. The king saw only his beloved son. But history would reveal something much greater. This child would grow up, leave behind a life of comfort, search for truth, attain enlightenment, and become the Buddha. When we look back at the signs surrounding his birth, they seem almost like a preview of the life that was to come.
The light that filled the sky can symbolize the wisdom that would later illuminate the darkness of ignorance. The heavenly music can symbolize the harmony and peace brought by his teachings. The flowers falling from above can symbolize the compassion that would spread throughout the world. The gentle rain can symbolize the cleansing of suffering and confusion. In this way, every symbol in the birth story points toward the Buddha's future mission. For me, one of the most touching aspects of the story is the reaction of the people who were present. Imagine being one of the attendants in Lumbini Garden that day. You begin the morning as part of an ordinary journey. You expect nothing unusual. Then suddenly, you find yourself witnessing an event that will be remembered thousands of years later. At that moment, they could not know that millions of people around the world would one day celebrate this birth. 
They could not know that temples would be built, teachings would spread across continents, and generations of people would find comfort and wisdom through the Buddha's words. Yet they stood there at the very beginning of that story. Today, more than twenty-six centuries later, people from many different countries, cultures, and backgrounds still gather to celebrate the Buddha's birth. This tells us something remarkable. Kings and kingdoms have disappeared. Ancient cities have changed. Powerful empires have risen and fallen. 
Yet the memory of the Buddha's birth remains alive. Why? Because people do not remember him simply as a historical figure. They remember him because his teachings continue to help human beings deal with the same challenges we face today—fear, anger, stress, attachment, and suffering. The story of his birth is therefore not only about the past. 
It is also about the present. Every time we develop kindness instead of hatred, a little of the Buddha's spirit is born within us. Every time we choose wisdom instead of ignorance, we follow the path that began in Lumbini Garden. And every time we bring peace to our own hearts and to others, we honor the meaning of the Buddha's birth. This is why Buddhists around the world continue to celebrate Vesak, the day of the Buddha's birth. It is not merely a celebration of an event that happened long ago. It is a reminder of the potential that exists within every human being—the potential to awaken, to love, to understand, and to bring light into the world.

After the extraordinary events in Lumbini Garden, the royal procession faced an important decision. The original plan had been for Queen Maya to continue her journey to her parents' home in Devadaha, where she would give birth according to the custom of that time. But now everything had changed. The child had already been born. There was no longer any reason to continue the journey. The attendants carefully prepared the procession to return to Kapilavastu, the capital city of the Sakya kingdom. At first glance, this may seem like a simple detail in the story. Yet when we think about it more deeply, we can appreciate how difficult the situation must have been. Queen Maya had just given birth. Even today, mothers need time to rest and recover after childbirth. Yet more than 2,600 years ago, she had to travel by cart over rough roads immediately after giving birth. The roads were uneven and uncomfortable. 
There were no modern hospitals, no ambulances, and no advanced medical care. The journey back to the capital must have required tremendous strength. When I reflect on this part of the story, I often think about the sacrifices that mothers make for their children. Throughout history, countless mothers have endured pain, hardship, and uncertainty to bring new life into the world. Queen Maya's experience reminds us of the courage and strength that mothers possess. Meanwhile, several royal messengers were sent ahead on horseback to bring the news to King Suddhodana. Imagine their excitement. They raced toward the capital carrying news that would change the future of the kingdom. The king's long-awaited son had finally been born. As the messengers approached the palace, word quickly spread throughout the city. 
The king had an heir. The royal family had welcomed a prince. Celebration filled the capital. When the royal procession finally returned to Kapilavastu, King Suddhodana came out together with his ministers and members of the royal court to welcome them. Like any father who has been waiting anxiously for the birth of his child, his heart was filled with anticipation. 
Then he saw the infant prince. According to traditional accounts, the child possessed an extraordinary beauty unlike anything people had seen before. There seemed to be a special dignity in his appearance. Many people felt that this was no ordinary child. The king gently took his son into his arms. Imagine that moment. For years he had hoped for a worthy successor. Now he was holding the child who would one day inherit his kingdom. 
Like every parent, he began dreaming about the future. Perhaps he imagined his son becoming a powerful ruler. Perhaps he imagined him protecting the kingdom and bringing prosperity to the people. Perhaps he imagined teaching him the skills of leadership and preparing him to one day sit upon the throne. The king's joy must have been beyond words.
 Yet life often unfolds in ways we do not expect. Sometimes the future holds something far greater than our personal plans and wishes. Soon after the prince's birth, King Suddhodana invited a renowned spiritual sage named Asita to the palace. Asita was not an ordinary man. He was widely respected for his spiritual achievements, deep meditation, and wisdom. Many people believed that through his spiritual practice he had developed extraordinary insight into human destiny. Because of his reputation, the king wanted him to examine the newborn prince.
 Perhaps the king expected to hear words of blessing. Perhaps he hoped Asita would confirm that the child would become a great king. When the old sage arrived at the palace, he was brought before the infant prince. The room became quiet. Everyone waited. Asita carefully looked at the child. He studied him with great attention. Minute after minute passed. The old sage remained focused. The king watched closely. At first he felt calm. But as time passed, he noticed something strange. Asita's face became increasingly serious. His expression grew more intense.Instead of smiling, he appeared deeply absorbed in thought.The king began to feel uneasy. "What does he see?" the king may have wondered. "Is there some danger ahead?" The atmosphere in the room became tense. Everyone waited for the sage to speak. 
Then something completely unexpected happened. mAsita began to cry. Imagine how shocking this must have been. This was not an emotional young man. This was a spiritual master who had spent decades training his mind. He was more than ninety years old. He had experienced much of life and cultivated deep inner stability. Yet now tears streamed down his face. The king was terrified. A thousand fears must have rushed into his mind. Was there some terrible fate awaiting his son? Would the prince die young? Would disaster strike the kingdom? Unable to remain silent, the king immediately asked: "Venerable sir, please tell me honestly.
Is there some misfortune that will happen to my son?" The old sage looked at the king and gently shook his head. "No, Your Majesty," he replied. "There is no misfortune." The king felt a small sense of relief. But he still did not understand why the sage was crying. Then Asita spoke words that would echo throughout history. "This child is destined for greatness beyond imagination. If he remains in worldly life, he will become a great universal monarch, a ruler whose influence will extend across the world. But if he leaves worldly life and follows the spiritual path, he will become a fully enlightened Buddha who will guide countless beings out of suffering." The room fell silent. These were extraordinary words. The king had expected a prediction of greatness. But he had not expected something so profound. 
A universal king would be admired and remembered for generations. Yet a Buddha would become a teacher for humanity itself. The prediction described a destiny far beyond political power. Then Asita added something even more surprising. "In my understanding, the prince will most likely choose the spiritual path." These words touched the king's heart in a very different way. Everyone else in the room may have been filled with wonder. But the king was a father. And fathers often have dreams for their children. He wanted a son who would inherit the throne. He wanted a prince who would continue the royal lineage. Now he was hearing that his beloved child might someday leave the palace behind. He might give up wealth, power, and status. He might walk away from everything the king hoped to give him. 
As a father, this was difficult to hear. In many ways, King Suddhodana's reaction is deeply human. Even today, parents often create plans for their children. They dream about successful careers, stable lives, and bright futures. But sometimes a child's true purpose turns out to be very different from what the parents imagined. The king's struggle reminds us that genuine love often means allowing others to follow their own path. After making his prediction, Asita continued to look at the prince. Then his tears returned. The king, now somewhat calmer, asked another question. "If no misfortune awaits my son, why do you continue to cry?" The old sage answered with words that many Buddhists have found deeply moving for centuries. 
"I am crying for myself." The king was surprised. For himself? What did that mean? Asita explained that he was already very old. He could clearly see that this child would one day become a Buddha and bring a path of wisdom and liberation to the world. But he also knew that he would not live long enough to hear those teachings. He would die before the prince reached enlightenment. He would never have the opportunity to sit before the Buddha and learn from him. That was the reason for his tears. The tears of Asita were not tears of fear. They were not tears of sadness for the prince. They were tears of longing. Tears born from wisdom. Before leaving the palace, Asita called for his nephew. According to tradition, he gave him a simple but important instruction. "When this prince grows up and begins teaching, go and learn from him." What a remarkable request. The old sage was already preparing the next generation. Even though he himself would not meet the future Buddha, he wanted someone he loved to benefit from those teachings. In this way, Asita's story becomes more than a prophecy.
 It becomes a lesson in humility. A truly wise person is not concerned with personal importance. A truly wise person simply wants truth and wisdom to continue benefiting others. Asita recognized greatness when he saw it. And rather than seeking honor for himself, he encouraged others to learn from it. As the old sage departed from the palace, life gradually returned to normal. The celebrations continued. The kingdom rejoiced. 
The infant prince slept peacefully in the royal palace. Yet unknown to most people, the future of humanity had already begun to change. The baby resting in his cradle would one day leave the palace, seek the truth, attain enlightenment beneath the Bodhi Tree, and become the Buddha. The old sage had seen a glimpse of that future. And his tears remain one of the most beautiful and moving moments in the entire story of the Buddha's birth.

As the days passed, joy continued throughout the kingdom. The birth of the prince had brought happiness not only to King Suddhodana and the royal family, but also to many people in the kingdom. A new heir had been born, and hopes for the future were high. 
According to the customs of ancient India, an important ceremony was held on the seventh day after the birth of a child. This was the naming ceremony, a special occasion where family members, spiritual leaders, and respected elders gathered together to bless the child and offer their observations about the child's future. When the day arrived, the infant prince was brought into the royal hall. The king invited seven respected sages and spiritual experts to examine the child. These men were known for their wisdom and their ability to recognize special qualities in people. Although they were not considered to possess the extraordinary spiritual insight of the sage Asita, they were still highly respected throughout the region. 
One by one, they carefully observed the young prince. According to ancient Buddhist tradition, they examined what are known as the "Thirty-Two Marks of a Great Being" and the "Eighty Minor Characteristics." These were physical signs that, according to traditional belief, indicated an extraordinary destiny. Today, modern people may not focus much on such physical signs. However, what is important is not the details themselves, but what they represented. The sages believed that certain qualities visible in the prince reflected inner greatness, wisdom, compassion, and leadership. As they examined him, they spoke about the prince's remarkable appearance. They described features that they believed pointed toward an exceptional future. After carefully discussing their observations, all seven sages arrived at the same conclusion. The prince would follow one of two paths. If he remained in worldly life, he would become a great ruler, a king of extraordinary influence and power. 
But if he chose the spiritual path, he would become a fully enlightened Buddha who would bring wisdom and liberation to countless beings. Notice something interesting here. The prediction made by the seven sages was essentially the same as the prediction given earlier by Asita. Different people, observing the same child, arrived at the same conclusion. 
This strengthened the belief that the prince's life would be unlike any ordinary life. After the ceremony, the child was given the name Siddhattha, or Siddhartha in Sanskrit. The name carries a beautiful meaning. It means "the one who achieves his goal," or "the one whose wishes are fulfilled." At first, this may seem like a simple name. But when we look back at history, it feels almost prophetic. The prince would indeed achieve his goal. 
However, the goal he eventually pursued was not wealth, power, or political success. His goal became the search for truth. His goal became understanding the causes of suffering and discovering a path to freedom. And after years of effort, meditation, and self-discovery, he achieved that goal completely. In this sense, the name Siddhattha became perfectly fulfilled.
 As we come to the end of the story of the Buddha's birth, I would like to reflect on what it means for us today. Many people hear stories about the Buddha's birth and focus on the miraculous events. They remember the heavenly music, the flowers, the light in the sky, and the prophecies. These stories are beautiful and inspiring. But I believe the deepest lesson lies elsewhere. The most important thing is not that extraordinary signs appeared when the Buddha was born. The most important thing is what he later became. After all, many children are born into the world every day. What made Siddhattha special was not simply his birth. 
It was the way he lived his life. He grew up surrounded by wealth and comfort, yet he was not satisfied with a life devoted only to pleasure and success. He looked deeply into the realities of life. He saw sickness. He saw old age. He saw death. He saw suffering. And instead of turning away from these realities, he chose to face them directly. He dedicated his life to finding answers to questions that affect every human being. Why do we suffer? Why do we experience fear, loss, and dissatisfaction? Is lasting peace possible? Can human beings become free from greed, hatred, and ignorance? The Buddha's greatness came not from supernatural power. His greatness came from his courage to search for truth and his compassion to share what he discovered with others. This is why his birth continues to be celebrated more than twenty-six centuries later. People do not honor him simply because he was born. 
They honor him because his life became a gift to humanity. There is another lesson hidden within this story. When Prince Siddhattha was born, no one around him could fully see what he would become. The attendants saw a baby. The king saw a son. The people saw a prince. Only later did the world come to understand his true significance. This reminds us of something important. Every human being carries hidden potential. When we look at a child, we cannot know what kind of person he or she may become. Within every child may exist future wisdom, kindness, creativity, or greatness. In fact, Buddhism teaches that every person possesses the potential for awakening. Perhaps not everyone will become a Buddha. 
But everyone can become wiser. Everyone can become more compassionate. Everyone can become more peaceful. Everyone can learn to understand themselves and help others. This message is especially meaningful today. We live in a world filled with technology, information, and material comfort. Yet many people still struggle with stress, loneliness, anxiety, anger, and dissatisfaction. The same human problems that existed during the Buddha's time still exist today. This is why the Buddha's teachings remain relevant. His message was never limited to one culture, one country, or one historical period. His message speaks directly to the human heart. It teaches us how to develop mindfulness, kindness, wisdom, and inner peace. 
And perhaps that is why the story of his birth continues to inspire people around the world. The celebration of the Buddha's birth is not merely about remembering an event from the distant past. It is also an opportunity to reflect on our own lives. Are we becoming kinder? Are we becoming wiser? Are we learning to understand ourselves more deeply? Are we bringing more peace into the world around us? These are questions that each of us can ask ourselves. The Buddha's birth reminds us that transformation is possible. A human being can grow beyond ignorance. A human being can develop extraordinary compassion. 
A human being can awaken to a deeper understanding of life. And because the Buddha was born as a human being, his story gives hope to all of us. His life tells us that wisdom is possible. Peace is possible. Compassion is possible. Awakening is possible. As we celebrate the birth of the Buddha, may we not only remember the child who was born in Lumbini Garden long ago. May we also remember the qualities that he later embodied—wisdom, compassion, courage, patience, and understanding. And may those qualities continue to grow within our own hearts. Thank you very much for listening. May you be well, may you be peaceful, and may the light of wisdom continue to guide your life.

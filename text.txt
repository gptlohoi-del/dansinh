Part 1: Why Are We Here Today?
Good evening everyone.
Before we begin talking about Buddhism, I would like to ask a simple question.
Have you ever met someone who seemed to have everything, but still did not seem happy?
Perhaps that person had a successful career. Perhaps they had money, a good education, or many followers on social media. From the outside, life looked wonderful. Yet inside, something was missing.
I think many of us have seen this. Some of us may even have experienced it ourselves.
To understand this better, let me tell you a real story about a young man named Tim Bergling. The world knew him as Avicii, one of the most successful music stars in history.
By his mid-20s, Tim had everything that many people dream of. He had millions of dollars. He had millions of followers on social media. His music was played in every country, and he performed in front of giant, cheering crowds. From the outside, his life looked perfect.
But inside, Tim was deeply unhappy.
The pressure of being famous and always staying connected to the world made him feel exhausted and lonely. He suffered from severe anxiety and stress. He once said that he was running a race that he could not win. In 2018, at the young age of 28, Tim chose to leave this world. After his death, his family shared that Tim was a sensitive soul who was constantly searching for answers about life, happiness, and meaning. He simply could not find peace.
Tim’s story is a tragedy, but it is also a powerful reminder.
Today, we live in a world that is more connected than ever before. We can speak to people on the other side of the planet in seconds. We have access to endless information. Technology helps us do things that previous generations could hardly imagine.
And yet, many people still struggle with stress, loneliness, anxiety, anger, and a feeling that something is not quite right.
A few years ago, a teacher was asked a very interesting question:
"What worries you most about young people today?"
Many people expected the teacher to talk about grades, technology, or the job market.
Instead, the teacher answered:
"Young people today know more information than ever before, but many are forgetting how to care deeply about other people."
That answer stayed with me.
Because perhaps the greatest challenge of our time is not a lack of knowledge.
Perhaps it is a lack of wisdom.
Not a lack of information.
But a lack of understanding.
Not a lack of success.
But a lack of inner peace.
When we look honestly at our lives, we realize that human beings have been asking the same questions for thousands of years.
How can we live with less suffering?
How can we find lasting happiness?
How can we become better human beings?
How can we raise children who are not only successful, but also kind, responsible, and compassionate?
More than twenty-six centuries ago, these same questions existed.
People experienced fear.
People experienced conflict.
People experienced loss.
People searched for meaning.
And it was into that world that a remarkable event took place.
In a beautiful garden called Lumbini, a prince was born who would later become known throughout the world as the Buddha.
For Buddhists, this was not simply the birth of an extraordinary teacher.
It was the appearance of a great light of wisdom and compassion in the world.
Tonight, I would like to share two stories.
The first is the story of the Buddha's birth.
The second is the story of how those teachings can still help us raise wiser, kinder, and more compassionate human beings today.
And perhaps, by the end of our time together, we may discover that the Buddha's birth is not only a story from the past.
It is also a reminder of what is possible within every human heart.

Part 2: The World Before the Buddha
To understand why the birth of the Buddha was so significant, it helps to imagine the world before that event.
When many people hear the phrase "ancient India," they often picture a distant world that has little connection to modern life. They imagine different clothing, different customs, different kingdoms, and different ways of living.
And in many ways, that is true.
But if we look a little deeper, we discover something surprising.
Human beings have not changed very much.
More than 2,600 years ago, people wanted happiness just as we do today.
Parents worried about their children.
Young people dreamed about the future.
Families struggled with disagreements.
Communities faced conflict.
People experienced illness, aging, loss, and uncertainty.
In other words, although the world looked different on the outside, the human heart was facing many of the same challenges that we face today.
At that time, there were many teachers, philosophers, and spiritual traditions. People were searching for answers. They wanted to understand life. They wanted to understand suffering. They wanted to know whether lasting peace was possible.
Yet confusion was common.
Many people followed beliefs simply because everyone around them believed the same thing.
Others followed leaders because they spoke confidently.
Some pursued wealth, believing that money would solve every problem.
Others pursued power, believing that control would bring happiness.
But even then, many discovered the same thing that people discover today:
External success does not always create inner peace.
We can see this clearly in our own time.
A person may have a successful career and still feel empty.
A student may receive excellent grades and still feel overwhelmed.
Someone may have thousands of followers online and still feel lonely.
Someone may own a beautiful house and still struggle to sleep peacefully at night.
This reminds us that human suffering is not only about lacking material things.
Often it comes from confusion, attachment, fear, anger, and endless craving for more.
A famous story tells of a businessman who spent his entire life trying to become rich. Year after year, he worked longer hours, accumulated more possessions, and achieved greater success.
One day, after finally reaching his financial goals, a friend asked him,
"Are you happy now?"
The businessman paused and answered honestly:
"I thought happiness would be waiting for me when I arrived. But every time I reached a goal, another goal appeared."
Many people can relate to that feeling.
We keep thinking:
"When I earn a little more, then I'll be happy."
"When I get promoted, then I'll be happy."
"When I find the perfect relationship, then I'll be happy."
"When my problems disappear, then I'll be happy."
But happiness keeps moving further away, like the horizon.
There is an old story about a man who spent his entire life waiting for happiness.
When he was a student, he said,
"I'll be happy when I graduate."
After graduation, he found a job and said,
"I'll be happy when I get promoted."
After the promotion came another goal.
Then another.
Then another.
Years passed.
He got married.
Bought a house.
Built a successful career.
Raised a family.
And each time he reached a milestone, he told himself:
"Just one more step, and then I'll finally be happy."
One evening, after retiring, he sat quietly on his porch and reflected on his life.
For the first time, he saw a pattern.
For decades, he had been preparing to live.
But he had forgotten to actually live.
Happiness had always been pushed into the future.
It was always waiting somewhere beyond the next achievement.
Perhaps many of us have experienced something similar.
We keep running toward happiness, only to discover that it keeps moving further away.
The Buddha would later help humanity see this pattern very clearly.
Not by asking people to reject life.
Not by asking people to escape the world.
But by helping people understand their minds more deeply.
And perhaps that is why the appearance of the Buddha was so important.
The world did not simply need another king.
It did not simply need another warrior.
It did not simply need another philosopher.
The world needed someone who could help people understand the causes of suffering and the path to inner peace and enlightenment.
When we look at today's world, perhaps we need that wisdom more than ever.
Technology can connect us across continents, but it cannot automatically create kindness.
Education can fill our minds with information, but it cannot automatically create wisdom.
Economic growth can improve our standard of living, but it cannot automatically teach us the art of being content with what we have.
Interestingly, modern research points in a similar direction.
One of the longest studies ever conducted on human happiness began at Harvard University in 1938.
Researchers followed hundreds of people throughout their lives.
For more than eighty years, they tried to answer a simple question:
"What truly makes a good life?"
Many expected the answer to be wealth.
Others expected fame or professional success.
But after decades of research, the conclusion was surprisingly different.
The strongest predictor of long-term happiness was not money, status, or achievement.
It was the quality of human relationships.
People who maintained healthy relationships tended to be happier, healthier, and more resilient throughout life.
In other words, even modern science has discovered something that spiritual traditions have emphasized for centuries:
A meaningful life is not built only on what we have.
It is built on how we live, how we relate to others, and how we cultivate our inner world.

The questions remain:
How do we deal with anger?
How do we respond to loss?
How do we overcome fear?
How do we become better human beings?
More than 2,600 years ago, a child was about to be born who would dedicate an entire life to exploring those questions.
And the story begins with a remarkable dream experienced by Queen Maya.
That dream would become the first chapter in one of the most influential spiritual stories in human history.

Part 3: The Birth at Lumbini Garden
Now let us travel back more than 2,600 years.
Long before modern cities, long before airplanes and smartphones, there was a kingdom in northern India known as the Sakya Kingdom.
The ruler of this kingdom was King Suddhodana. The queen was Queen Maya.
According to Buddhist tradition, before the Buddha's birth, Queen Maya experienced a remarkable dream.
One night, the queen dreamed of a beautiful white elephant descending gently from the heavens. The elephant carried a white lotus flower and approached with peace and dignity. In the dream, it entered the queen's side, and at that moment she felt a deep sense of joy, purity, and serenity.
When the dream was interpreted, wise people in the kingdom believed it was a sign that an extraordinary being would soon be born.
Of course, people today may view this story in different ways.
Some may understand it literally.
Others may see it as a symbolic story.
But regardless of how we interpret it, the message is beautiful.
Sometimes humanity is blessed by the arrival of individuals whose lives bring wisdom, compassion, and hope to countless others.
As the time of birth approached, Queen Maya followed an ancient custom and began a journey to her parents' home.
Along the way, the royal procession arrived at a beautiful garden called Lumbini.
Imagine that place for a moment.
Tall trees swaying gently in the breeze.
Birds singing among the branches.
Flowers blooming in every direction.
The fresh fragrance of nature filling the air.
No grand palace.
No crowded city.
No ceremony prepared by human hands.
Only the quiet beauty of nature.
And it was there, in Lumbini Garden, that one of the most remembered events in human history took place.
For Buddhists around the world, this is more than just the arrival of a new life.
It is the appearance of a Great Awakened One who would later dedicate an entire life to understanding suffering and showing others the path to peace.
What I find especially meaningful is the setting of this event.
He was not born within the comforts of a palace, nor in a modern facility built for birth.
The birth took place in a garden.
A place filled with life, beauty, and harmony.
Perhaps this reminds us of something important.
Human beings often believe that greatness begins with power.
But many of the most transformative things in life begin quietly.
A kind word.
A compassionate action.
A moment of understanding.
A decision to forgive.
A decision to help another person.
These moments may appear small, but they can change lives.
History often remembers great events.
Yet, many of the currents that shape our world unfold silently, almost unnoticed.
The birth of the Buddha is one example.
On that day, the people present could not have known that the teachings of this newborn prince would one day reach every continent on Earth.
They could not have known that millions of people would gather thousands of years later to celebrate Vesak.
They could not have known that the message of compassion, mindfulness, and wisdom would inspire people across cultures, languages, and generations.
There is another aspect of this story that speaks powerfully to our modern world.
Today, many people feel discouraged when looking at the problems around them.
We see conflict.
We see division.
We see environmental challenges.
We see anxiety and loneliness.
Sometimes it feels as if the world is becoming darker.
But the story of the Buddha's birth reminds us that even during difficult times, great goodness can appear.
Hope can appear.
Wisdom can appear.
Compassion can appear.
A single heart can make a difference.
Not necessarily through wealth.
Not necessarily through power.
But through understanding and kindness.
When Buddhists celebrate Vesak, they are not only remembering something that happened long ago.
They are celebrating the possibility that wisdom can arise in this world.
That compassion can arise in this world.
And that every human being has the potential to cultivate those qualities.
The birth of the Buddha is therefore more than a historical event.
It is a symbol of humanity's potential.
It reminds us that beneath confusion, fear, and selfishness
There is also the possibility of awakening.
There is also the possibility of wisdom.
There is also the possibility of a heart filled with compassion for all living beings.
And that possibility is what we celebrate today.
Part 4: A Different Kind of Greatness

When people hear about the birth of Prince Siddhartha, they often wonder:
What made this life so special?
After all, history has seen many princes.
Many kings.
Many generals.
Many famous leaders.
Most of them are remembered because they gained power, conquered territories, accumulated wealth, or changed the course of nations.
But the Buddha's story is different.
The greatness we remember today was not built on military victories.
It was not built on political power.
It was not built on wealth.
It was built on wisdom, compassion, and a deep understanding of life.
And perhaps that is one reason why the Buddha's life continues to inspire people across cultures and generations.
Think about the messages we often hear in today's world.
Children are frequently asked:
"What do you want to be when you grow up?"
The answers are usually familiar.
A doctor.
A lawyer.
A business owner.
An engineer.
A celebrity.
There is nothing wrong with any of these dreams.
They can all be wonderful paths.
But there is a question that is asked much less often.
Not:
"What do you want to do?"
But:
"What kind of person do you want to become?"
Those are very different questions.
One focuses on success.
The other focuses on character.
One focuses on achievement.
The other focuses on values.
And if we are honest, many of society's problems are not caused by a lack of intelligence.
They are caused by a lack of wisdom.
The world has many intelligent people.
Yet intelligence alone does not prevent greed.
It does not prevent cruelty.
It does not prevent dishonesty.
It does not automatically create compassion.
A person can be highly educated and still hurt others.
A person can be wealthy and still be unhappy.
A person can be successful and still feel empty inside.
This is why the Buddha's life offers such a powerful lesson.
The Buddha showed that the highest achievement is not simply becoming successful.
The highest achievement is transforming the heart.
Several years ago, a university conducted a study asking elderly people what mattered most in their lives when they looked back.
Very few people said,
"I wish I had spent more time at the office."
Very few said,
"I wish I had made more money."
Instead, most spoke about relationships.
Kindness.
Love.
Meaning.
The people they helped.
The people who helped them.
In other words, when life is nearly over, many people discover that what truly matters is not what they owned.
It is who they became.
And this brings us back to the Buddha.
When Buddhists celebrate Vesak, they are not celebrating the birth of a prince simply because that prince would become famous.
They are celebrating the birth of a life that demonstrated extraordinary wisdom and compassion.
A life dedicated to helping others understand suffering and find peace.
A life that reminds us that true greatness is measured differently.
Not by how many people serve us.
But by how many people we serve.
Not by how much we possess.
But by how much goodness we bring into the world.
Not by how loudly our name is remembered.
But by how deeply our actions benefit others.
This understanding is especially important when we think about the next generation.
As parents, teachers, grandparents, or community members, we naturally want children to succeed.
We want them to be healthy.
We want them to have opportunities.
We want them to have a good future.
But perhaps we should ask ourselves an additional question:
Are we helping them become good human beings?
Because a child may become successful without becoming kind.
A child may become wealthy without becoming compassionate.
A child may become powerful without becoming wise.
The Buddha's life encourages us to aim higher.
To help young people develop not only knowledge, but also character.
Not only skills, but also compassion.
Not only ambition, but also responsibility.
And this leads us to an important question.
If we want to raise wise and compassionate human beings, what qualities should we nurture?
What values truly help children flourish?
The answer may be simpler than we think.

# Part 5: What Kind of Children Does the World Need Today?
So far, we have reflected on the birth of the Buddha and a different understanding of greatness.
Now let us bring this discussion closer to everyday life.
Many people ask:
"What is the most important thing we can give to a child?"
Some may say education.
Others may say financial security.
Others may say opportunities.
And all of these things are valuable.
But if we look carefully, we can find examples of highly educated people who are dishonest.
We can find wealthy people who are unhappy.
We can find talented people who use their abilities in harmful ways.
This suggests something important.
Knowledge alone is not enough.
Success alone is not enough.
Children need character.
They need values that help them become not only capable people, but good people.
Today I would like to share four simple qualities that are deeply connected with Buddhist teachings and are just as relevant today as they were 2,600 years ago.
## 1. Respect
The first quality is respect.
Respect may sound simple, but it is becoming increasingly rare.
We live in a culture where people are encouraged to express opinions quickly.
Social media allows anyone to criticize almost anyone.
Sometimes people speak before listening.
Sometimes people judge before understanding.
Yet every healthy relationship begins with respect.
Respect for parents.
Respect for teachers.
Respect for friends.
Respect for people who think differently from us.
Respect for nature.
Respect for life itself.
Several years ago, a school principal was asked what quality best predicted future success.
The answer was not intelligence.
It was not talent.
It was the ability to respect others and work well with them.
Think about your own life.
Who do you trust most?
Usually it is not the smartest person in the room.
It is the person who treats others with dignity.
The person who listens.
The person who speaks kindly.
The person who makes others feel valued.
When children learn respect, they develop the foundation for every other virtue.
Respect teaches humility.
Respect teaches gratitude.
Respect teaches self-control.
And perhaps most importantly, respect reminds us that every person we meet is carrying a story we may not fully understand.
## 2. Compassion
The second quality is compassion.
If respect helps us see the value of others, compassion moves us to help them.
Several years ago, a teacher noticed that one student often came to school without lunch.
The child never complained.
Never asked for help.
One day another student quietly began bringing an extra sandwich every morning.
No announcement.
No photographs.
No social media post.
Just a simple act of kindness.
Years later, the teacher said that this small act influenced the entire classroom.
Children began helping one another more often.
A culture of kindness started to grow.
That is the power of compassion.
One small action can spread much further than we imagine.
The Buddha taught compassion not because it is morally impressive.
The Buddha taught compassion because compassion reduces suffering.
When we help others, we improve the world around us.
But something else happens.
Our own hearts become lighter as well.
Modern research often confirms what spiritual traditions have taught for centuries.
People who regularly help others often report greater life satisfaction and stronger emotional well-being.
Compassion benefits both the receiver and the giver.
In a world that often encourages competition, compassion reminds us that we rise higher when we help one another.
## 3. Humility and Lifelong Learning
The third quality is humility.
Humility does not mean thinking poorly of ourselves.
It means understanding that there is always more to learn.
One of the greatest dangers in life is believing that we already know enough.
Many breakthroughs in science happened because someone was willing to say:
"I might be wrong."
Many conflicts in families continue because nobody is willing to say:
"I need to listen more carefully."
Many opportunities are missed because people stop learning.
Children naturally ask questions.
Why is the sky blue?
Why do birds fly?
Why do people get old?
Unfortunately, many adults lose that curiosity.
The Buddha's life encourages us to remain students throughout life.
No matter our age.
No matter our profession.
No matter our achievements.
There is always more wisdom to discover.
A humble person keeps growing.
A proud person stops growing.
And growth is one of the greatest gifts we can give ourselves.
## 4. Responsibility
The final quality is responsibility.
Responsibility means understanding that our choices matter.
Many years ago, a businessman was asked about the most important lesson he learned during his career.
He replied:
"The day I stopped blaming others was the day my life began to improve."
That answer contains great wisdom.
It is easy to blame circumstances.
To blame parents.
To blame society.
To blame other people.
But growth begins when we ask:
"What can I do?"
Responsibility means admitting mistakes.
Learning from mistakes.
Trying again.
It means keeping promises.
It means caring about how our actions affect others.
The Buddha taught that actions have consequences.
hether we call it karma, responsibility, or simple cause and effect, the principle is familiar toall of us.
Our actions shape our future.
The habits we practice today become the character we carry tomorrow.
And this is why responsibility is such a powerful quality for young people.
It teaches that life is not merely something that happens to us.
Life is something we help create through our choices.
When children learn responsibility, they become stronger.
When they learn compassion, they become kinder.
When they learn humility, they keep growing.
When they learn respect, they build healthy relationships.
Together, these qualities form the foundation of a meaningful life.
And perhaps this is one of the greatest lessons we can learn from the Buddha's example.
A good life is not built in a single day.
It is built one thought, one action, and one choice at a time.

Part 6: What the Buddha Offers Modern Life
At this point, some people may be thinking:
“The story is beautiful.”
“The values are inspiring.”
“But what does all this have to do with my life today?”
After all, we live in a very different world.
Most of us are not kings or queens.
We do not live in ancient India.
We have jobs, families, deadlines, bills, emails, and endless notifications on our phones.
Life moves fast.
Very fast.
In fact, one of the biggest challenges of modern life is not a lack of information.
It is a lack of peace.
Many people wake up and immediately check their phones.
Messages.
News.
Emails.
Social media.
By the time they get out of bed, their minds are already racing.
And the cycle continues throughout the day.
More information.
More pressure.
More comparison.
More distraction.
Yet despite all of our technology, many people feel exhausted.
Not physically.
Mentally.
Emotionally.
Spiritually.
Several years ago, a university student was asked what caused the most stress in life.
The answer was surprising.
The student did not say exams.
Did not say money.
Did not say family problems.
The student said:
“I never feel like my mind gets a chance to rest.”
Perhaps many of us can relate to that.
The body may be sitting quietly in a room.
But the mind is somewhere else.
Thinking about yesterday.
Worrying about tomorrow.
Comparing ourselves to others.
Replaying old conversations.
Imagining future problems.
Always moving.
Rarely resting.
This is where the Buddha’s teachings become incredibly practical.
Not because they give us more information.
But because they help us relate differently to our own minds.
Today, I would like to mention three simple gifts that the Buddha offers modern life.
Not just to Buddhists.
But to anyone.
The First Gift: Awareness
Many people go through life on autopilot.
We eat without paying attention.
We listen without really hearing.
We walk without noticing where we are.
Sometimes we become so busy that we stop experiencing our own lives.
Awareness changes that.
Awareness simply means being present.
Being here.
Being awake to this moment.
A famous story tells of a traveler who spent years searching for happiness.
The traveler crossed mountains, visited distant countries, and met many teachers.
Finally, one teacher asked:
“When you drink tea, do you taste the tea?”
The traveler laughed.
“Of course.”
The teacher smiled.
“Then why are you always thinking about somewhere else while drinking it?”
Sometimes wisdom is surprisingly simple.
Life is happening now.
Not yesterday.
Not tomorrow.
Now.
Awareness helps us return to that reality.
The Second Gift: Compassion
The modern world often teaches competition.
Be faster.
Be stronger.
Be more successful.
Be better than everyone else.
Competition has its place.
But if competition becomes the center of life, people eventually become isolated.
The Buddha taught something different.
The Buddha taught that our lives are connected.
The happiness of one person affects others.
The suffering of one person affects others.
We are not as separate as we sometimes imagine.
A few years ago, during a difficult winter storm, neighbors in one community began helping elderly residents with groceries and transportation.
Something interesting happened.
The people receiving help felt grateful.
But the people giving help also reported feeling happier.
Why?
Because human beings are naturally designed for connection.
Compassion is not merely a moral duty.
It is part of what makes us human.
In a world where loneliness is becoming increasingly common, compassion may be more important than ever.
The Third Gift: Wisdom
Perhaps the greatest gift offered by the Buddha is wisdom.
Wisdom is different from knowledge.
Knowledge tells us how to build a faster computer.
Wisdom tells us how to use it well.
Knowledge tells us how to make money.
Wisdom tells us what money can and cannot do.
Knowledge helps us change the world around us.
Wisdom helps us understand the world within us.
Many people spend their entire lives climbing a ladder.
Only to discover that the ladder was leaning against the wrong wall.
They achieve what they wanted.
But not what they truly needed.
Wisdom helps us ask deeper questions.
What really matters?
What kind of person am I becoming?
What will remain after success, status, and possessions are gone?
The Buddha encouraged people not to accept ideas blindly.
Not even religious ideas.
Instead, to observe.
To reflect.
To understand.
To see clearly.
And perhaps that is why these teachings continue to speak to people from every culture and every generation.
Because the deepest human questions have not changed.
We still seek happiness.
We still seek meaning.
We still seek peace.
And the Buddha’s teachings offer a path toward all three.
Not through blind belief.
Not through rituals alone.
But through understanding ourselves more deeply.
Through cultivating awareness.
Through practicing compassion.
Through developing wisdom.
These teachings began more than 2,600 years ago.
Yet they remain remarkably relevant today.
Because although the world has changed, the human heart remains much the same.
And that brings us to one final reflection.
If the Buddha’s birth represents the possibility of wisdom, compassion, and awakening in the world, how can we honor that message in our own lives?

Part 7: Bringing the Light Forward
As we come to the end of our journey today, I would like to share one final story.
Several years ago, a father was walking home with his young daughter after attending a community event.
It was evening.
The sky was growing dark.
As they walked, they passed hundreds of small candles that had been lit for a celebration.
The little girl stopped and looked at them carefully.
Then she asked her father,
“Dad, which candle is the most important one?”
The father smiled.
He looked at the many candles glowing in the darkness.
Then he replied,
“The most important candle is the one that lights another candle.”
The little girl thought about that answer for a moment.
Then she quietly continued walking.
I have always loved that story.
Because it reminds us that true greatness is not about shining alone.
It is about helping others shine as well.
When we reflect on the birth of the Buddha, we are not simply remembering an event from long ago.
We are remembering a life that brought light into the world.
A light of wisdom.
A light of compassion.
A light of understanding.
And perhaps the most beautiful part of this story is that this light was never meant to belong to only one person.
The Buddha did not teach people to admire wisdom from a distance.
The Buddha encouraged people to cultivate wisdom themselves.
The Buddha did not ask people merely to praise compassion.
The Buddha encouraged people to practice compassion in daily life.
The Buddha did not teach that peace belongs only to monks, nuns, or spiritual teachers.
The Buddha showed that peace can begin in every human heart.
It can begin in a family.
It can begin in a classroom.
It can begin in a workplace.
It can begin in a simple conversation.
And sometimes it begins with very small actions.
Listening patiently.
Speaking kindly.
Admitting a mistake.
Offering help.
Showing gratitude.
Forgiving someone.
These actions may seem ordinary.
Yet they are often the very actions that change lives.
Earlier today, we spoke about the world our children are growing up in.
A world of incredible technology.
A world of rapid change.
A world filled with opportunity, but also uncertainty.
In such a world, children certainly need knowledge.
They need skills.
They need education.
But perhaps they need something even more important.
They need wisdom to make good choices.
They need compassion to care about others.
They need courage to do what is right.
They need inner peace to remain steady when life becomes difficult.
These are gifts that no machine can provide.
These are qualities that must be cultivated within the human heart.
And this is one reason why the celebration of Vesak remains so meaningful.
The birth of the Buddha reminds us of what humanity can become at its best.
Not perfect.
Not powerful.
Not famous.
But wise.
Compassionate.
Awake.
When people first hear the story of the Buddha’s birth, they sometimes think it is a story about the past.
But perhaps it is also a story about the future.
Because every generation faces the same question:
What kind of human beings will we become?
Will we become people who add more anger to the world?
Or more understanding?
More division?
Or more kindness?
More selfishness?
Or more compassion?
Each of us answers that question through our daily actions.
Not once.
But every day.
So as we celebrate Vesak together, perhaps we can leave with one simple reflection.
The greatest way to honor the birth of the Buddha is not merely to remember it.
The greatest way to honor the birth of the Buddha is to bring more wisdom, more compassion, and more kindness into the world around us.
If each of us lights even one small candle of goodness, that light can spread far beyond what we imagine.
And just as one candle can light another, and another, and another, the influence of a single kind action can continue long after we are gone.
More than 2,600 years ago, a child was born in a peaceful garden called Lumbini.
Today, that story continues to inspire millions of people around the world.
May it also inspire us.
To live with greater wisdom.
To love with greater compassion.
And to become a source of light for others.
Thank you very much.
Happy Vesak to everyone.
May you be peaceful.
May you be happy.
May you be well.
